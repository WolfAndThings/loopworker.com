The Problem

Your property looks better in person than it does online. That's costing you bookings.

Architectural photography is brutally expensive

A professional architectural photographer charges $10,000-20,000 for a full property shoot. That's before retouching, before licensing, before you realize you need drone shots too. Most properties do one big shoot every few years and squeeze those same images across every channel until they feel stale.

Seasons change, your photos don't

Your website shows the pool in summer. It's November. Your Instagram hasn't been updated since the renovation. The spa got remodeled three months ago and you're still showing the old tile. Guests arrive expecting one thing and find another. That's how you earn a three-star review.

Multiple platforms, no system

Booking.com, Expedia, your own website, Instagram, Facebook, Google Business. Each platform has different image specs, different aspect ratios, different expectations. Someone on your team is manually resizing and uploading images across six platforms. That's not a content strategy. That's a chore.

You're selling rooms, not experiences

A bed, a desk, a bathroom. That's what your listing shows. But nobody books a hotel room for the desk. They book for the rooftop at sunset, the restaurant on the ground floor, the feeling of walking through the lobby. Your content needs to sell the dream, not the square footage.

Case Study

A luxury resort that looks like it was shot by a film crew.

A luxury cave resort in Southeast Asia needed visual content that matched the impossibility of the property itself — soaring vaulted ceilings, glass walls looking out over jungle canopy, clean wood bedframes built into carved stone walls. The kind of place that looks like it shouldn't exist. But their existing photography was flat. Wide-angle lens distortion, harsh flash, every room looking the same.

We built a visual system around cinematic Kodak Gold 200 film stock — warm pastels, natural light pouring through glass, the kind of golden-hour glow that makes stone and wood feel alive. No flash. No artificial staging. Architecture as the subject, with guests small in frame to sell the scale of the space.

Every room type, every amenity, every common area — photographed in the same cinematic language. Polished stone, brass fixtures, clean wood — all rendered with the kind of warmth and texture that makes you feel the humidity through the screen. Lifestyle scenes of guests at breakfast overlooking the valley, reading in a carved alcove, walking through an open-air corridor.

The property's booking page went from functional to aspirational overnight. Their Instagram became a destination in itself. Every image tells the same story: this place is real, and you should be here. The system regenerates seasonal content — monsoon atmosphere, dry season golden light — without scheduling another photographer.
